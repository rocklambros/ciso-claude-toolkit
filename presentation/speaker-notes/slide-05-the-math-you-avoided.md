# Slide 5: The math you avoided

Let me run two scenarios side by side. Same organization. Same data types. Different tools.

Scenario one: email. Your organization sends approximately 200 emails per day that contain files shared externally. Attachments going to clients, partners, vendors. Each email with an external attachment is an opportunity for misdirected PII, wrong attachment, wrong recipient, or sensitive data in a file that should not have left the building. At a 0.1% error rate per external share, which is conservative based on DBIR data for human error, you run 200 external shares per working day times 250 working days. That is 50,000 exposure opportunities per year. The annualized probability of at least one data exposure event: approximately 18%. Your organization has been running at roughly 18% annual probability of an email-driven data exposure for years. You accepted that risk. You may not have quantified it, but you accepted it by keeping email turned on.

Scenario two: a governed LLM deployment. Fifty users, each interacting with the model five times per day. Same 0.1% error rate per interaction, meaning the probability that a single interaction results in sensitive data being exposed to an unauthorized party through prompt leakage, context window extraction, or model memorization. Fifty users, five interactions, 250 working days: 62,500 interactions per year. The annualized probability: approximately 5%.

Read those two numbers again. Email: 18%. LLM: 5%. The LLM scenario is three to four times less likely to produce a data exposure event than the email scenario you already accepted.

Why? The difference is structural. Email has an external distribution path baked into its design. Every email with an external recipient is a data package leaving your perimeter. The LLM, in a governed deployment, does not have an external distribution path. The data goes to the model API and the response comes back. There is no "send to wrong recipient" failure mode. There is no "attach the wrong file" failure mode. The exposure paths that exist, prompt injection, context window extraction, model memorization, are real, but they are lower probability than "human clicks the wrong name in the To field," which happens in every organization every week.

The math is not making an argument. The math is describing a comparison. If your risk tolerance accepts email at 18%, it is hard to justify blocking an LLM at 5% without explaining what makes the LLM risk categorically different. If you believe it is categorically different, put a number on it. Show the failure mode that changes the calculation. That is a conversation I want to have. But "it feels riskier" is not a risk assessment, and we do not let our boards make decisions on feelings about any other technology.

I am not telling you to approve every AI deployment. I am telling you to measure the risk the same way you measure every other technology risk and make the decision based on the measurement.
