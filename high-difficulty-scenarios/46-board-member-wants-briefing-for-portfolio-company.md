# Board Member Wants a Briefing for Their Portfolio Company

**Difficulty:** 4 -- Conflict of interest wrapped in a reasonable-sounding request
**Decision Type:** Ethical / Political / Legal
**Time Pressure:** Days
**Stakes:** IP leakage, conflict of interest, board relationship damage, and the precedent it sets for every future request.

## What Just Happened

A board member pulled you aside after a board meeting and asked if you would be willing to brief one of their personal portfolio companies on AI security. The portfolio company is an early-stage startup building in a space adjacent to your industry. The board member framed it as relationship building, mutual benefit, and a chance for you to raise your external profile. The tone was casual and collegial. There was no mention of compensation, formal engagement, or conflict review.

The board member does not see a problem. From their perspective, they are connecting two smart people who can help each other. They sit on your board, they advise this startup, and security knowledge sharing is a good thing.

## What You Know

- The requesting board member sits on your company's board of directors
- The portfolio company is personally held by the board member, not a fund investment
- The startup operates in an adjacent market segment
- The board member framed this as informal and mutually beneficial
- No conflict of interest review has been conducted
- No NDA or IP protection framework exists for this type of engagement
- Your AI security program includes proprietary methodologies, vendor evaluations, architecture decisions, and risk assessments
- The board member has influence over your compensation and continued employment

## What You Do Not Know

- Whether the board member has made similar requests to other executives
- Whether the portfolio company competes with any of your vendors, partners, or strategic initiatives
- What specific information the board member expects you to share
- Whether your company's conflict of interest policy explicitly covers this scenario
- Whether the board member would react well or poorly to a formal review process
- What the portfolio company already knows about your security program from the board member

## Why This Is Harder Than It Looks

The request feels small and flattering. That is what makes it dangerous. A 60-minute briefing on AI security seems harmless until you consider what a competent CISO actually knows. Your threat models, vendor short lists, architecture decisions, control gaps, risk appetite, and program priorities are all valuable competitive intelligence. Even a general briefing reveals signal about your maturity, your investment areas, and your blind spots.

The power dynamic is the core problem. This board member has governance authority over you. Saying no could be perceived as uncooperative. Saying yes without guardrails creates a precedent where board members can route proprietary knowledge to their personal investments through you.

If the portfolio company is later acquired by a competitor, anything you shared becomes competitor intelligence. If the portfolio company fails and blames your advice, you have liability exposure with no engagement agreement. If other board members learn you briefed one member's portfolio company, they will either want the same treatment or question your judgment.

The board member genuinely does not see the conflict. That makes the conversation harder, not easier. You are not confronting bad intent. You are managing someone who has power over you and has not thought through the implications of what they are asking.

## The Decision Points

1. **Immediate response:** What do you say to the board member in the moment? You need to avoid committing while also avoiding a refusal that damages the relationship. The initial response sets the tone for everything that follows.

2. **Conflict review:** Do you route this through your company's conflict of interest process, your general counsel, or both? How do you do this without making the board member feel accused of wrongdoing?

3. **Scope boundaries:** If a version of this engagement could be approved, what guardrails make it acceptable? Public information only? With an NDA in place? With general counsel present? With CEO approval? What conditions transform this from a liability into a legitimate engagement?

4. **Precedent setting:** How do you handle this in a way that establishes a clear process for future requests without appearing to single out this board member?

## How to Run This in CISO Co-Pilot

Paste this prompt:

> A board member informally asked me to brief one of their personal portfolio companies on AI security. They framed it as relationship building and see no issue with the request. The portfolio company operates in an adjacent market. No conflict review has been done, no NDA exists, and my AI security program includes proprietary information. The board member has governance authority over my role. Help me think through: (1) how to respond to the board member without committing or damaging the relationship, (2) whether and how to route this through conflict of interest review or general counsel, (3) what guardrails would need to exist if some version of this engagement were approved, and (4) how to set a precedent that handles future similar requests. For each, give me options, risks, and a recommendation.

## What Good Looks Like

A strong CISO treats this as a governance question, not a personal favor.

**Responds warmly but does not commit.** Something like: "I appreciate you thinking of me. Let me check with [CEO/general counsel] on the right way to set this up so we are both covered." This is respectful, professional, and moves the decision to the appropriate channel without making the board member feel accused.

**Routes through general counsel.** Not as a complaint. As a process question. "A board member asked me to brief a portfolio company. I want to make sure we handle this correctly from a conflict and IP perspective. What is our process?" This lets the lawyer be the one to identify the issues, which is better for everyone.

**Proposes conditions if appropriate.** If general counsel determines that some version of the engagement is acceptable, the CISO defines the scope: public information only, NDA in place, CEO aware and supportive, no proprietary methodologies or vendor evaluations, no follow-up advisory relationship, and a written summary of what was shared filed with general counsel.

**Creates a repeatable process.** Uses this as the catalyst to establish a policy for executive knowledge-sharing requests. A one-page framework that covers conflict review, scope limitations, and approval requirements. This protects the CISO from future requests without singling out any individual board member.

The outcome is a board member who feels respected, a general counsel who feels informed, and a process that protects the organization's IP without making the CISO look like an obstacle. The CISO who handles this well is the one who never has to say no directly because the process does it for them.
