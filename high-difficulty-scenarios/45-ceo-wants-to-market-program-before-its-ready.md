# CEO Wants to Market the Security Program Before It Is Ready

**Difficulty:** 4 -- Political navigation with reputational and regulatory risk
**Decision Type:** Political / Ethical
**Time Pressure:** Days (event is next week)
**Stakes:** Public claims about security maturity that cannot be substantiated create regulatory exposure, customer trust risk, and personal liability for the CISO.

## What Just Happened

The CEO secured a keynote speaking slot at a major industry conference next week. The theme is AI security leadership. The draft talking points position your organization as having a mature, operational AI security program. The CEO wants to announce specific capabilities: model risk governance, automated bias detection, adversarial testing, and continuous monitoring of AI systems in production.

The problem is that three of those capabilities have open critical findings. Model risk governance has an unresolved gap in third-party model inventory. Bias detection is running but producing false negatives at an unacceptable rate. Adversarial testing exists as a pilot, not an operational program. The CEO sees direction and ambition. You see a gap between what is being claimed and what is true.

The CEO does not understand why you are pushing back. From his perspective, the company is investing heavily, the team is building real capability, and being first to claim leadership in this space has enormous market value. He views your objections as perfectionism getting in the way of business opportunity.

## What You Know

- The keynote is scheduled for next week at a major industry conference
- Draft talking points claim operational maturity in four AI security capabilities
- Three of those capabilities have open critical findings documented in your risk register
- The CEO is personally invested in this speaking opportunity
- Competitors have made similar claims with less substance behind them
- Your risk register and audit findings are discoverable in litigation or regulatory inquiry
- The CEO has not consulted legal or compliance on the specific claims

## What You Do Not Know

- Whether the CEO would adjust claims if he understood the specific gaps
- Whether competitors' public claims are being scrutinized by regulators
- How customers or prospects would react to the specific claims if they could see your internal findings
- Whether the board has been briefed on the current state of AI security maturity
- How the CEO would respond to a flat refusal versus a proposed alternative

## Why This Is Harder Than It Looks

This is not a technical disagreement. It is a political problem with legal consequences. The CEO is not lying on purpose. He genuinely believes that investment and direction equal maturity. Many executives think this way. The CISO who simply says "we cannot say that" without offering an alternative will be overruled and marginalized.

The real risk is not the conference itself. It is what happens after. Public claims about security capabilities become part of the company's representations to customers, regulators, and investors. When a breach occurs or a regulator audits the AI program, those claims will be measured against reality. The gap between the keynote and the risk register becomes evidence of either negligence or dishonesty.

If the CISO gets overruled and the claims go forward, the CISO now owns a documentation problem. The risk register says one thing. The CEO said another thing publicly. If the CISO stays silent, that silence can be interpreted as endorsement. If the CISO objects in writing, that memo becomes discoverable evidence that the company knew its claims were inaccurate.

The competitor argument is a trap. "They are saying it too" does not reduce your exposure. It increases it, because regulators tend to make examples.

## The Decision Points

1. **Framing the conversation:** How do you present the gap to the CEO in a way that he will hear? Leading with risk will sound like obstruction. Leading with opportunity may get his attention. The framing determines whether you have influence or get sidelined.

2. **Proposing an alternative:** Can you give the CEO something he can say that is true, compelling, and differentiated without overstating maturity? The goal is a version of the keynote that the CEO is excited about and that you can stand behind.

3. **Documenting your position:** If the CEO proceeds with claims you cannot support, how do you protect yourself and the organization? A formal memo to the CEO? A note to general counsel? An update to the risk register? Each option has different consequences.

4. **Escalation threshold:** At what point does this become a board-level issue? If the CEO makes public claims that contradict documented findings, is the CISO obligated to inform the audit committee?

## What Good Looks Like

A strong CISO does not show up with a list of reasons the CEO cannot do what he wants. A strong CISO shows up with a version of the keynote that works.

**Reframes the narrative.** Instead of claiming mature capabilities, position the company as transparent about its AI security journey. "Here is what we are building, here is why it matters, and here is what we have learned" is more credible and more differentiated than "we have solved AI security." Audiences and analysts increasingly reward honesty over posturing.

**Gets specific about what is true.** If the organization has invested in AI security headcount, built a governance framework, and started adversarial testing, those are real accomplishments worth talking about. The CISO helps the CEO tell that story accurately.

**Documents the conversation.** A brief written summary to the CEO after the meeting: "Per our discussion, here is the revised language I can support and here are the specific claims I recommend we avoid given our current findings." This is professional, not adversarial, and it creates a record.

**Knows the escalation line.** If the CEO insists on claims that contradict the risk register, the CISO informs general counsel. Not as a threat. As a professional obligation. The general counsel needs to understand the liability exposure of public claims that are contradicted by internal documentation. Most general counsels, once they see the gap, will handle the CEO conversation themselves.

The outcome is a keynote that makes the CEO look good and does not create a liability file. That is the CISO adding value, not blocking it.
