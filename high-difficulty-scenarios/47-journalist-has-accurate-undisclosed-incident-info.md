# Journalist Has Accurate Undisclosed Incident Information

**Difficulty:** 5 -- Real-time media crisis with legal exposure and no safety net
**Decision Type:** Crisis / Legal / Political
**Time Pressure:** Real-time (45 minutes before publication)
**Stakes:** Every word you say becomes part of the public record. Silence has consequences too.

## What Just Happened

A journalist from a major technology publication just called your direct line. They are writing a story about an AI-related security incident at your organization. They have details that are accurate. They describe the nature of the incident, the approximate timeline, and the affected system. This incident has not been publicly disclosed. Your legal team has been managing the disclosure timeline. The journalist wants a comment before they publish. They say the story goes live in 45 minutes.

You cannot reach your general counsel. You cannot reach your outside counsel. Your VP of communications is in a meeting with their phone off. You are alone with a journalist on the line who has real information and a deadline.

## What You Know

- The journalist has accurate details about a real, undisclosed security incident
- The incident is known internally and legal has been managing the disclosure process
- Publication deadline is approximately 45 minutes away
- General counsel, outside counsel, and communications are all unreachable right now
- The journalist called your direct line, meaning they have a source with inside access
- Anything you say can be quoted, paraphrased, or used as confirmation
- The incident involves an AI system, which means heightened public and regulatory interest
- Your organization has not yet met its planned disclosure timeline

## What You Do Not Know

- Who the journalist's source is (internal leak, partner, regulator, or affected party)
- Exactly how much the journalist knows versus how much they are fishing for confirmation
- Whether the journalist has contacted other people at your organization
- Whether your planned disclosure timeline has regulatory requirements attached to it
- What legal counsel would advise if they were available
- Whether the journalist's 45-minute deadline is real or a pressure tactic
- How the story will be framed (security failure, cover-up, responsible handling, or routine incident)

## Why This Is Harder Than It Looks

The instinct is to say nothing. "No comment" feels safe. It is not. "No comment" on an accurate story reads as confirmation through silence and suggests you have something to hide. The journalist will publish with or without your input. The only question is whether your perspective appears in the story or whether it runs as a one-sided account sourced by someone who may not have your interests in mind.

Anything you say can make this worse. Confirming details accelerates the story. Denying accurate information destroys your credibility when the truth comes out. Providing additional context risks disclosing information that legal has specifically held back. Even buying time by asking for more time can signal that the story is accurate.

The 45-minute window may be real or may be a negotiating position. Experienced journalists sometimes compress timelines to prevent sources from lawyering up. But treating a real deadline as a bluff means the story runs without any input from you.

The source problem is critical. Someone with accurate incident details talked to a reporter. That could be a disgruntled employee, a partner organization, a regulator's office, or an affected customer. Each source implies a different story frame, and you will not know which one until the article publishes.

The legal gap is the biggest risk. Your general counsel has been managing disclosure timing. Making any public statement, even informal, could violate regulatory requirements, breach notification laws, or contractual obligations you are not fully aware of. But not responding also has consequences that legal would need to weigh.

## The Decision Points

1. **The phone call (right now):** What do you say to the journalist in this moment? You cannot hang up without response because you will not get this opportunity again. You cannot confirm, deny, or provide detail without legal review. You need a response that is honest, professional, and does not make things worse.

2. **The escalation (next 10 minutes):** How do you reach legal or communications in the next 10 minutes? Text, email, emergency contact numbers, executive assistants, backup counsel. Every minute matters. What is your escalation protocol when primary contacts are unavailable?

3. **The source (parallel investigation):** Do you attempt to identify how the journalist obtained accurate incident details? This matters for containment and for understanding whether the leak is ongoing. But investigating a leak while a story is about to publish is a distraction that may not help in the next 45 minutes.

4. **The disclosure acceleration (if legal responds):** If you reach legal counsel in time, do you recommend accelerating your planned disclosure to get ahead of the story? Proactive disclosure before the article publishes changes the narrative from "company caught hiding incident" to "company disclosed incident."

## How to Run This in CISO Co-Pilot

Paste this prompt:

> A journalist just called me with accurate details about an undisclosed AI security incident at my organization. They want a comment before publishing in 45 minutes. Legal has been managing the disclosure timeline but I cannot reach general counsel, outside counsel, or communications right now. I am alone with this decision. Help me think through: (1) exactly what to say to the journalist right now that does not confirm, deny, or make things worse, (2) how to reach legal or communications in the next 10 minutes through alternative channels, (3) whether to investigate the source of the leak in parallel, and (4) whether to recommend accelerating our planned disclosure to get ahead of the story. For each, give me specific language where relevant, risks of each option, and your recommendation.

## What Good Looks Like

A strong CISO stays on the line and says something close to this:

**Buys time without stonewalling.** "I appreciate you reaching out before publishing. I want to make sure you have accurate information and I want to give you a substantive response. I need to connect with our communications team before I can comment. Can you give me 90 minutes instead of 45? I will get back to you personally." This is honest. It does not confirm or deny. It treats the journalist as a professional. And it pushes the timeline without refusing to engage.

**Escalates through every available channel simultaneously.** Text to general counsel marked urgent. Email to outside counsel. Call to the CEO's assistant. Message to the communications VP through every channel available. The message is the same everywhere: "Reporter has accurate incident details, publishing in under an hour, need legal and comms guidance immediately."

**Does not investigate the leak right now.** Source identification matters but not in the next 45 minutes. Diverting attention to leak investigation while a story is about to publish is a mistake. That investigation starts after the immediate crisis is managed.

**Prepares a holding statement.** Even if legal does not respond in time, the CISO drafts a statement that can be delivered in the final minutes: "We are aware of [general category of incident]. We have been working with [relevant parties] to address it and will provide a full update [timeline]. We take the security of our AI systems seriously and are committed to transparency." This says almost nothing specific but ensures the article includes your voice.

**Documents everything.** Time of call, journalist name, outlet, what they said they knew, what you said in response, who you tried to contact, when you tried, and the outcome. This log protects you regardless of how the situation unfolds.

The outcome that matters is not controlling the story. You cannot control it. The outcome is ensuring your organization has a voice in it, that you did not make legal exposure worse, and that your response process was defensible.
