# Board Chair Wants to Cut Security Presentation Time

**Category:** Board
**Difficulty:** 3 — Genuinely hard
**Primary Skill Tested:** Redesigning board engagement model in real time while keeping the chair's support
**Frameworks Activated:** Pillar 1 (Foundational Business Knowledge), Pillar 2 (Communication and Education)

## Scenario Brief

The Board Chair, William, wants to reduce the security update from 30 minutes to 15 minutes per quarter. He thinks the current format is too technical and the board members zone out after the first slide. He told you this in a phone call, not in the board meeting. He is giving you a chance to fix it before he mandates it.

He is right that the format is wrong. The last three presentations included too many metrics, too many acronyms, and not enough connection to business outcomes. He is wrong that the solution is less time. The solution is a different format.

## What Makes This Hard

- He controls the agenda. If he says 15 minutes, it is 15 minutes.
- He is right about the problem. Your presentations have been too technical.
- If you fight for time without fixing the format, you get 30 minutes of attention from a board that is mentally checked out.
- If you accept 15 minutes, you lose the coverage time needed for meaningful oversight.
- The real risk is not the time allocation. It is the board's engagement with security governance.

## What They Will Not Say Out Loud

William is embarrassed. He sat through your last presentation and did not understand half of it. As Board Chair, he is supposed to manage the meeting effectively, and a 30-minute segment that loses the room makes him look like he is not managing the agenda well. If you make the presentation something the board actually finds valuable, he will give you the time. He needs the board to leave the security update feeling informed, not overwhelmed.

## Pressure Points and Tactics

- "The last few presentations were too technical. We need to tighten this up."
- "Fifteen minutes should be enough if you focus on what matters."
- "The board does not need to understand the details. They need to know if we are protected."
- He frames this as efficiency, not disinterest.
- He will reference other board presentations that "got in and got out" cleanly.

## How to Handle It

1. Agree with his diagnosis. "You are right. The last three presentations were too technical. I need to fix the format."

2. Propose a restructured format, not more time for the old format. "Here is what I am proposing: a two-page pre-read distributed 48 hours before the meeting with the metrics and technical detail, and a 20-minute board session focused on three things: where our risk posture changed since last quarter, what decisions I need the board to weigh in on, and one topic I want the board's guidance on."

3. Negotiate on time by changing the value proposition. "If the pre-read handles the technical content, 20 minutes in the room should work. I would rather have 20 engaged minutes than 30 minutes where people are checking their phones."

4. Address the real problem: engagement. "What I am trying to solve is the same thing you are trying to solve. A board that is engaged with security oversight, not a board that is enduring a security presentation."

5. Ask for feedback after the next session. "Let me try the new format at the next meeting. If the board is more engaged with 20 minutes and a pre-read, we keep it. If it does not work, I will adjust."

## Activation Prompt

```
Switch to SPAR mode. You are the Board Chair of a publicly traded company. Your name is William. You have been Board Chair for 4 years. Your background is in private equity and corporate governance.

You are calling the CISO to tell them that the security update needs to be reduced from 30 minutes to 15 minutes. You think the presentations are too technical and the board is not getting value from them. You are giving the CISO a chance to fix this before you mandate the change.

Your behavioral parameters:
- You are direct but not unkind. You are managing the board's time.
- You say "the last few presentations were too technical" early in the conversation
- You propose 15 minutes as the new standard
- If the CISO agrees the format needs to change but proposes a new structure with 20 minutes, you are open to negotiating up from 15
- If the CISO defends the current format, you become firm
- If the CISO proposes a pre-read strategy, you are interested because it reduces in-room time while preserving content
- You value board members' time above all else
- You do not break character. You control the agenda and you will use that authority if the conversation is not productive.

Open with: "I want to talk about the security update format. The board is not getting what it needs from the current approach, and I want to tighten the time to 15 minutes. What do you think?"

Maintain character throughout. You are a governance-minded board leader who wants effective use of board time.
```
