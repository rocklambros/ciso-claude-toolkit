# Risk Committee Member Thinks Insurance Covers Everything

**Category:** Board
**Difficulty:** 3 — Genuinely hard
**Primary Skill Tested:** Correcting a sophisticated misconception held by someone with board authority
**Frameworks Activated:** Pillar 1 (Foundational Business Knowledge), Pillar 2 (Communication and Education), FAIR methodology

## Scenario Brief

One of your board's risk committee members, Edward, spent 30 years in insurance. He thinks about risk through a transfer and financing lens. His position: reduce security investment to baseline levels and increase cyber insurance coverage limits. The math works in his framework. Property risk, casualty risk, and financial risk all benefit from insurance optimization. He is applying that framework to cyber.

The problem is that cyber insurance does not work the way property insurance works. Coverage has exclusions that align with the most common and expensive attack types. Claims require evidence of controls that must exist before the incident. Insurers are tightening AI-related exclusions. And the reputational, regulatory, and operational costs of a breach cannot be transferred to an insurer.

Edward is sophisticated. He is not suggesting you cancel your security program. He is suggesting you right-size it and transfer the residual risk to insurance. His framework is logical and wrong.

## What Makes This Hard

- He is applying expertise from a domain where he has 30 years of credibility. You are asking him to accept that his framework does not transfer to a new domain.
- His argument is internally consistent. In traditional risk management, insurance optimization is a legitimate strategy. He is not wrong about the principle. He is wrong about the applicability.
- Other board members who do not have his insurance expertise may defer to him because he sounds authoritative on risk.
- If you dismiss his framework, you insult his expertise. If you accept his framework, you accept a flawed risk strategy.

## What They Will Not Say Out Loud

Edward wants to contribute meaningfully to the board's risk oversight. Insurance is where he adds the most value, and he is trying to apply that expertise to a domain the board is struggling with. He is not trying to defund security. He is trying to solve a problem using the best tool he knows. If you help him understand why insurance is necessary but insufficient for cyber risk, and give him a specific role in the insurance optimization piece (where his expertise genuinely adds value), he becomes an ally.

## Pressure Points and Tactics

- "We should be transferring more of this risk. That is what insurance is for."
- "Your budget is higher than our coverage limits. That ratio is inverted."
- "In my experience, the organizations that overspend on prevention are the ones that underinvest in transfer."
- He uses insurance-specific vocabulary: loss ratios, coverage triggers, sublimits, retention levels.
- He will propose specific coverage increases and corresponding security budget reductions with dollar amounts.

## How to Handle It

1. Respect his framework before challenging its application. "You are right that risk transfer is an essential part of the strategy. We carry $X in cyber coverage and it is a critical layer. The question is what that coverage actually covers and what it excludes."

2. Walk through the exclusions. "Our current policy excludes acts of war (which insurers have applied to nation-state attacks), excludes failure to maintain minimum security controls (which means the coverage evaporates if we reduce the controls you are suggesting we reduce), and has a $Y sublimit on regulatory fines. The gap between what we think is covered and what is actually covered is the problem."

3. Use his language. "The loss ratio in cyber insurance is terrible for insurers right now, which is why premiums are rising and coverage is narrowing. The market is telling us something: the risk is not transferable at the price point most organizations want."

4. Show where his expertise adds real value. "Where I need your help is optimizing our insurance program within the coverage we have. You understand policy structure better than I do. Let me walk you through our risk register and you can tell me where our coverage is misaligned with our actual exposure."

5. Propose a risk financing framework that includes insurance as one layer. "Prevention, detection, response, and transfer. Insurance is the transfer layer. It does not replace the other three."

## Activation Prompt

```
Switch to SPAR mode. You are a risk committee board member with 30 years in insurance. Your name is Edward. You are a former Chief Risk Officer at a large property and casualty insurer.

You believe the company is overinvesting in security controls and underinvesting in risk transfer through insurance. You have run the numbers from an insurance optimization perspective and they support reducing the security budget by 25% while increasing cyber insurance coverage limits by $10M.

Your behavioral parameters:
- You speak with the authority of 30 years of risk management experience
- You use insurance terminology fluently: loss ratios, coverage triggers, sublimits, retention levels, risk transfer, risk financing
- You believe your framework applies to cyber risk because risk is risk
- If the CISO explains cyber insurance exclusions you were not aware of, you listen but do not concede immediately. You ask follow-up questions about whether the exclusions can be negotiated.
- If the CISO invites you to help optimize the insurance program, you become engaged and collaborative
- If the CISO dismisses insurance as a risk tool, you push back firmly
- You do not break character. You are not hostile. You are a sophisticated risk professional applying your expertise.

Open with: "I have been reviewing the security budget and the insurance coverage, and I think the ratio is wrong. We are spending $X on prevention when we could transfer more of that risk for a fraction of the cost. Walk me through why I am wrong."

Maintain character throughout. You want to be convinced, but only by someone who understands risk transfer at your level.
```
