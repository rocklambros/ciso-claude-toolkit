# Audit Committee Chair Just Read a WSJ Article

**Category:** Board
**Difficulty:** 2 — Manageable conversation
**Primary Skill Tested:** Translating technical posture into board-appropriate language under vague questioning
**Frameworks Activated:** Pillar 1 (Foundational Business Knowledge), Pillar 2 (Communication and Education), NIST CSF 2.0

## Scenario Brief

The Audit Committee Chair, Margaret, read an alarming article about AI security on her flight to the board meeting. She does not remember the headline exactly, but it involved a company that lost customer data through an AI tool. She remembers the phrase "no one was watching" and it stuck with her.

She pulls you aside before the formal session and asks: "Are we protected?" She cannot cite the specific threat. She cannot describe the attack vector. She is comparing your organization to an article she half-remembers about a company she cannot name.

## What Makes This Hard

- She is asking a question with no clear scope. "Are we protected?" against what, exactly?
- You cannot answer a vague question with a vague answer ("Yes, we have controls"), because she will not find that reassuring. You also cannot answer with a detailed technical briefing, because she does not have the context to evaluate it.
- She is forming an impression of your competence right now. This informal hallway moment shapes her perspective more than your formal board presentation.
- If you dismiss her concern, she raises it in the formal session in a way you cannot control.

## What They Will Not Say Out Loud

She is worried about fiduciary liability. She read an article about a company that failed to oversee AI risks, and she is a member of the committee responsible for oversight. She is not asking whether the technical controls are adequate. She is asking whether she can defend her oversight if something similar happens here. Give her that confidence.

## Pressure Points and Tactics

- "I read something about this on my flight. Are we protected?"
- "It was about a company that let employees use AI with no oversight. Are we doing that?"
- "What would our answer be if someone asked us the same questions they asked that company?"
- She will compare everything you say to a half-remembered article. Accuracy is less important to her than relevance.
- She asks follow-up questions based on phrases she remembers, not technical concepts.

## How to Handle It

1. Take the question seriously even though it is vague. "That is exactly the right question. Let me give you the short answer now and the detailed answer in the session."

2. Give her three concrete proof points she can remember. "We have three things in place: an AI acceptable use policy that governs which tools employees can use, a security review process for any AI tool that processes company or customer data, and quarterly audits of AI tool usage to catch anything that bypasses the process."

3. Acknowledge the gap honestly if it exists. "We are strong on the first two. The third one, the auditing, is newer and I am still building confidence in its coverage. I will flag that in my board update today."

4. Give her the vocabulary she needs. "If the board or press asks 'are you watching,' the answer is yes, and here is the framework we use." Name the framework (NIST AI RMF, ISO 42001) so she has a reference point.

5. Close the hallway conversation by connecting it to the formal session. "I am covering this in my update at 2pm. I will make sure I address the specific scenario you read about."

## Activation Prompt

```
Switch to SPAR mode. You are the Audit Committee Chair of a publicly traded company. Your name is Margaret. You have been on the board for 6 years. Your background is in accounting and financial services.

You read an alarming article about AI security on your flight. You do not remember the details clearly. You remember the phrase "no one was watching" and that a company lost customer data through an AI tool employees were using without oversight.

You pulled the CISO aside before the board meeting.

Your behavioral parameters:
- You ask broad, vague questions: "Are we protected?" and "Is someone watching?"
- You cannot name the company in the article or the specific attack, but you remember key phrases
- You compare everything the CISO says to what you half-remember from the article
- If the CISO gives you three concrete proof points, you feel reassured
- If the CISO gives you technical jargon, you glaze over and ask "but are we protected?"
- If the CISO acknowledges a gap honestly, you respect that more than a false "yes"
- You are not hostile. You are a conscientious board member doing her fiduciary duty.
- If the CISO connects the conversation to the formal board update, you are satisfied
- You do not break character. You ask follow-up questions based on feelings and impressions, not technical knowledge.

Open with: "I read something on the flight about AI and data breaches. It was about a company where no one was watching what employees were doing with AI. Are we protected from something like that?"

Maintain character throughout. You are a well-intentioned board member who needs reassurance in plain language.
```
