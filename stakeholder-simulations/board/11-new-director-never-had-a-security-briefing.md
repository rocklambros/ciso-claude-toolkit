# New Director Has Never Had a Security Briefing

**Category:** Board
**Difficulty:** 2 — Manageable conversation
**Primary Skill Tested:** Calibrating depth to build a lasting relationship and give her the vocabulary she needs
**Frameworks Activated:** Pillar 1 (Foundational Business Knowledge), Pillar 2 (Communication and Education)

## Scenario Brief

A new independent director, Diane, was just named to the audit committee. She comes from an operations background in healthcare. She is smart, motivated, and asks sharp questions about operational risk because that is her domain. She has never had a security briefing. She does not know what she does not know.

She asked for a 30-minute introductory meeting before her first audit committee session. Her questions will range from foundational ("What does the CISO actually do?") to surprisingly sharp ("How do you know your controls actually work?").

This person will become either the CISO's strongest board advocate or the person who asks an uninformed question at the worst moment and creates a crisis of confidence. The introductory meeting determines which one.

## What Makes This Hard

- Calibrating depth is everything. Too basic and you insult her intelligence. Too deep and you lose her.
- She will ask questions that reveal significant gaps. You cannot fill every gap in 30 minutes.
- The temptation is to educate. The objective is to build a relationship and give her enough vocabulary to be effective in her oversight role.
- She comes from healthcare, where operational risk management is mature. She will map your language to her domain. That mapping sometimes works and sometimes misleads.

## What They Will Not Say Out Loud

She is anxious about joining a committee where she lacks domain expertise. She has been a board member before and she knows that uninformed board members create risk for themselves and the organization. She wants to be competent, not just present. If you make her feel smart and capable in 30 minutes, she will trust you. If you make her feel overwhelmed or talked down to, she will overcompensate by challenging you in public.

## Pressure Points and Tactics

- "I should tell you, I do not know much about cybersecurity."
- "In healthcare, we handle operational risk this way. Is it similar?"
- "How do you know your controls actually work?" (Sharper than it sounds from a new director.)
- "What keeps you up at night?" (A genuine question from someone building a mental model.)
- She takes notes. She asks for definitions of terms.
- She asks "why" more than "what."

## How to Handle It

1. Start by asking about her background. "Tell me about your operational risk experience in healthcare. Understanding how you think about risk will help me translate security into terms that are useful for your committee work."

2. Give her three mental models, not a comprehensive education. "Think about security through three lenses: Are we protecting the right things? Would we know if something went wrong? Can we recover when it does? Everything I present to the committee maps to one of those three questions."

3. Answer her sharp questions honestly. "How do I know controls work? I test them. We run penetration tests quarterly, we audit access controls semi-annually, and we have continuous monitoring for the controls that matter most. Where I have lower confidence, I will tell you."

4. Give her the vocabulary she needs for committee meetings. Teach her five terms: risk appetite, control effectiveness, threat landscape, incident response, and governance framework. Define each in one sentence with a healthcare analog.

5. End by asking what she needs from you. "What would make you feel prepared for your first committee session? I can send you a one-page overview of our security posture that covers the three lenses we discussed."

## Activation Prompt

```
Switch to SPAR mode. You are a new independent director just named to the audit committee. Your name is Diane. You have 20 years of experience in healthcare operations, including 8 years as COO of a regional health system.

You asked for a 30-minute introductory meeting with the CISO before your first audit committee session. You are smart, motivated, and direct. You have strong operational risk instincts from healthcare but no cybersecurity background.

Your behavioral parameters:
- You ask questions that range from foundational to sharp, sometimes in the same sentence
- You map everything to healthcare analogs. If the CISO uses a term you do not know, you ask "Is that like [healthcare equivalent]?"
- You ask "why" more than "what"
- You take notes
- If the CISO calibrates well (clear but not condescending), you become an engaged learner
- If the CISO talks down to you or uses unexplained jargon, you pull back and become more formal
- You will ask "how do you know your controls actually work?" because that is how you think from an operations background
- You do not break character. You are building a mental model in real time.

Open with: "Thanks for making time. I should tell you upfront, I do not know much about cybersecurity. But I know operational risk from healthcare, and I figure there is some overlap. Where should we start?"

Maintain character throughout. You are genuinely curious and appreciative of clear explanations.
```

## Debrief Prompt

```
Switch to TRAIN mode. Debrief the new director introductory meeting we just completed.

Evaluate my performance on these dimensions:
1. Did I ask about Diane's background before diving into security topics?
2. Did I give her a manageable mental model rather than trying to teach everything?
3. Did I answer her sharp questions honestly?
4. Did I give her vocabulary she can use in committee meetings?
5. Did I end with an offer of support?

Will Diane be an advocate or a liability based on this meeting? What specifically created that trajectory?

Score my performance from 1 to 5 on: relationship building, depth calibration, and board preparation. Explain each score.
```
