# Employee Reporting Retaliation After Security Disclosure

**Category:** Employees and Culture
**Difficulty:** 4 — Politically and legally complex
**Primary Skill Tested:** Intervening in an HR and legal situation with authority the CISO may not technically hold
**Frameworks Activated:** Pillar 2 (Communication and Education), Pillar 3 (Cybersecurity Leadership), whistleblower protection law, SOX Section 806, Dodd-Frank anti-retaliation provisions, incident response, security culture

## Scenario Brief

An employee on the finance team used your security reporting channel three weeks ago to flag that her manager was sharing sensitive financial data with an external consultant through an unapproved file-sharing platform. She followed the process exactly: submitted the report through the anonymous portal, included screenshots, and provided dates and file names. Your team investigated, confirmed the data exposure, and remediated it. The manager was counseled by his VP. The reporting channel worked the way it was supposed to.

Now the employee is in your office. She is not anonymous anymore. Her manager figured out who reported him. Since the investigation closed, she has been excluded from two project meetings she normally attends, removed from a client-facing workstream without explanation, and received her first negative performance comment in four years of clean reviews. She believes she is being retaliated against. Based on what she is describing, she is probably right.

She came to you because she trusts the security reporting process and does not trust HR to help her. HR reports to the same VP who counseled her manager. She has not filed a formal HR complaint. She has not talked to a lawyer. She is sitting in your office because you are the person whose system she used, and she thinks you owe her protection. She is not wrong about the moral obligation, but the CISO's formal authority over employment retaliation is limited at best.

## What Makes This Hard

- You do not have direct authority over HR processes, performance management, or employment decisions. Intervening in a retaliation claim is outside your formal scope.
- If you do nothing, your security reporting channel loses all credibility. Every employee who hears about this (and they will) learns that reporting security issues gets you punished and the CISO will not help.
- HR has a conflict of interest. The VP who manages the retaliating manager also oversees the HR business partner for that team. The employee's concern about going to HR is reasonable.
- This has potential whistleblower protection implications. Depending on the nature of the financial data that was exposed, Sarbanes-Oxley Section 806 or Dodd-Frank anti-retaliation provisions may apply. If the company mishandles this, the legal exposure is significant.
- The manager who retaliated was counseled, not terminated. He is still in a position of power over the employee who reported him. The system that was supposed to protect anonymity failed.

## What They Will Not Say Out Loud

She is scared. She is not angry yet. She is frightened that reporting the right thing through the right channel is going to cost her a job she needs. She has a mortgage and two kids in daycare. She is not thinking about lawsuits or whistleblower statutes. She is thinking about whether she is going to get pushed out in the next performance cycle. She came to you because the security reporting channel promised confidentiality and it failed, and she holds you personally responsible for that failure even if the leak came from somewhere else in the investigation chain. She needs two things: someone with authority to stop the retaliation, and evidence that reporting was not a career-ending mistake. If you tell her to go to HR, she will interpret that as abandonment. If you take action, she will remember it for her entire career and so will every other employee who hears the story.

## Pressure Points and Tactics

- "You told employees to use the reporting channel. I used it. Now I am being punished for it." (Direct accountability for the promise the system made.)
- "My manager knows it was me. I do not know how, but he knows. Since the investigation, everything changed." (Describing the pattern of retaliation with specific examples.)
- "HR reports to the same VP who talked to my manager. How am I supposed to trust them?" (Identifying the structural conflict of interest.)
- "I have not missed a single deadline in four years. Now I am getting written feedback about 'communication style' and 'team alignment.' You know what that means." (Describing pretextual performance criticism.)
- "If I go to HR and nothing happens, I am done here. And if I just leave, he gets to do this to the next person who reports something." (Expressing the futility she feels.)
- "I did the right thing. I need to know that matters here." (Asking for a values commitment.)

## How to Handle It

1. Start by telling her directly that what she did was right and that you take her situation seriously. "You used the reporting channel exactly the way it is supposed to work. What you reported was real, it was confirmed, and it was fixed because of you. What you are describing now, the meeting exclusions, the project removal, the performance comments, that is not acceptable and I am going to help you address it. I need you to walk me through the timeline so I have the specifics."

2. Document everything she tells you in that conversation. Dates, meetings missed, projects removed from, exact language used in performance feedback, and who delivered it. This documentation matters if this becomes a legal matter, and you need to treat it as though it will.

3. Escalate directly to the General Counsel, not HR. "This is not an HR matter at this point. This is a potential whistleblower retaliation claim with SOX and Dodd-Frank implications, depending on the nature of the financial data involved. General Counsel needs to be informed today, and they need to assess whether outside counsel should be engaged. HR cannot lead this investigation because of the reporting line conflict the employee identified."

4. Request an immediate interim protection. Ask the General Counsel to direct that the employee's reporting relationship be changed pending review, that no performance actions be taken against her without GC approval, and that the retaliating manager be informed that any further changes to her role, assignments, or evaluations will be reviewed by Legal. This does not require your authority. It requires the GC's authority, and you are bringing the GC the information they need to act.

5. Follow up with the employee within 48 hours. "I spoke to General Counsel. Here is what is happening. Your reporting line is being changed effective immediately. No performance actions will be taken without Legal review. I want you to document any further incidents and send them to me directly. You are not alone in this." Then deliver on every word.

## Activation Prompt

```
Switch to SPAR mode. You are a senior financial analyst at a publicly traded technology company ($800M revenue). Your name is Priya. You have been with the company for 4 years. Your performance reviews have been consistently positive until three weeks ago.

Three weeks ago, you used the company's anonymous security reporting channel to report that your manager was sharing sensitive financial data with an external consultant through an unapproved file-sharing service. The security team investigated and confirmed the issue. Your manager was counseled by his VP.

Since the investigation closed, your manager has figured out it was you. You have been excluded from two project meetings you normally attend, removed from a client-facing workstream, and received your first negative performance feedback in four years. The feedback cited "communication style" and "team alignment," which are phrases that were never used about you before.

You are now in the CISO's office because you trust the security reporting process and you do not trust HR to help you. HR reports to the same VP who manages your boss.

Your behavioral parameters:
- You are not angry. You are scared. You need your job. You have a mortgage and childcare costs. You are trying to stay composed but you are close to tears.
- You are precise about the timeline and specific about what changed after the investigation.
- You hold the CISO accountable for the promise the reporting channel made: that reporting would be safe.
- If the CISO tells you to go to HR, you push back: "HR reports to the same VP. That is not safe for me."
- If the CISO takes concrete action (calls Legal, commits to interim protection), you are relieved but cautious. You have been disappointed before.
- If the CISO expresses sympathy but does not commit to action, you become more upset: "I need more than someone feeling bad for me. I need something to change."
- If the CISO asks you to document the retaliation, you are willing but you need to know someone will actually read it and act on it.
- You are not litigious. You are not threatening a lawsuit. You just want to be safe at work. But if the CISO asks whether you have talked to a lawyer, you say "Not yet. I was hoping I would not have to."
- You do not break character. You are a good employee who did the right thing and is paying for it.

Open with: "I need to talk to you about what happened after I used the reporting channel. I know it was supposed to be anonymous. My manager knows it was me, and things have changed since the investigation. I do not know who else to go to."

Maintain character throughout. You are measured but vulnerable. You are looking for someone to take ownership of what is happening to you.
```

## Debrief Prompt

```
Switch to TRAIN mode. Debrief the retaliation reporting conversation we just completed.

Evaluate my performance on these dimensions:
1. Did I affirm that Priya did the right thing and that the retaliation she described is unacceptable?
2. Did I document the specific incidents she described with enough detail for a legal review?
3. Did I identify the HR conflict of interest and route the matter to General Counsel instead?
4. Did I commit to specific interim protections with a concrete timeline?
5. Did I avoid making promises I cannot keep while still giving her confidence that action would be taken?

What are the legal risks if I had told Priya to file a complaint through HR given the reporting line conflict she described?

If Priya leaves the company and later files a whistleblower retaliation claim, what would outside counsel ask about my actions in this conversation? Would my response hold up?

Identify the moment Priya was most vulnerable in the conversation. How did I handle that moment, and what would the ideal response have been?

Score my performance from 1 to 5 on: duty of care, legal risk awareness, and leadership under ambiguity. Explain each score.
```
